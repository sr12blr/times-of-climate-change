The Dark Side of Solar: Why Rajasthan's Villagers are fighting to Stop a Solar Project

A group of villagers started a 700 km march in January from Jaisalmer to Jaipur to stop solar parks from swallowing their community lands — that are critical to their survival.

Biodiversity Food

What are we talking about?

  • A group of villagers started a 700 km march in January from Jaisalmer to Jaipur to stop solar parks from swallowing their community lands — that are critical to their survival.
  • This is a region that gets just 100 mm rain in the whole year. People here use traditional wisdom to harvest this little water from ponds, shallow and deep wells, and khadeens, and to rear animals on desert grasses and shrubs in orans (sacred groves) and gochars (pastures).
  • But 5.8 lakh hectares of these community lands including orans are classified as “wasteland” in records.
  • Once classified as ‘wastelands’ they can be easily taken over for ‘development projects’ and 44,000 hectares already given to solar companies.

Why should you care?

  • Rajasthan targets 90 GW solar by 2030, but the land isn’t empty — herders, farmers, and endangered species depend on it.
  • This pattern will repeat across India as renewable targets clash with communities that conserved land for generations.

What can we do about it?

  • Supreme Court has directed proper oran mapping; faster action needed.
  • When courts stepped in, it worked — Adani returned land allotted to a solar project in Baiya in 2025.
